## The Incident
January 28, 2023. The entire **Government Integrated Data Centre (GIDC)** went dark. A massive cyberattack took down hundreds of government sites. But the real chaos was at **Tribhuvan International Airport (TIA)**.

The immigration server crashed. International flights were grounded. Passengers were stuck in limbo because officers physically couldn't check visas or process entry logs.

## What Happened?
It wasn't a sophisticated hack; it was a brute force **Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS)**.

### The Mechanism
Attackers flooded the GIDC's connection with junk traffic. The GIDC's "pipe" got completely saturated. At the time, there was no robust upstream filtering or a cloud-based **Scrubbing Centre** to absorb the hit.

Because everything—from the Prime Minister's blog to the Airport's critical immigration database—sat behind the same choked gateway, *everything* went down. This is the definition of a "Single Point of Failure."

## Why It Mattered
This was more than an inconvenience. It was a national embarrassment and a security risk. It showed the world that Nepal's digital border could be closed by a botnet.

## The Takeaway
1.  **DDoS Mitigation is Mandatory**: You can't run critical infrastructure on a standard ISP line. You need dedicated scrubbing (like Cloudflare Magic Transit or Akamai).
2.  **Decouple Systems**: Why does the airport immigration system share bandwidth with public websites? Critical systems need their own dedicated, redundant lines.
3.  **Failover Drills**: We had backup links, but they didn't work when we needed them. If you don't test your failover processing under load, you don't actually have failover.
