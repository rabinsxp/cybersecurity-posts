## The Context
During the chaos of the COVID-19 pandemic, the **Covid-19 Crisis Management Centre (CCMC)** rolled out mandatory online forms for travelers. The goal was simple: track health status. The result? A massive aggregation of sensitive personal data—health history, citizenship numbers, and travel logs—collected in haste.

## The Incident
It didn't take long for security researchers to notice something alarming. The CCMC portal had gaping security holes. In one iteration, it was possible to view other people's submissions just by tweaking the URL parameters or hitting insecure API endpoints. This wasn't a complex hack; it was a fundamental oversight.

## Technical Analysis
### 1. Insecure Direct Object References (IDOR)
Much like other major breaches in Nepal, this system suffered from IDOR. If my form ID was `1001`, changing it to `1002` would often reveal someone else's data.

### 2. Encryption? What Encryption?
The breach raised serious questions about data at rest. There was little evidence that this highly sensitive health data was encrypted in the backend, meaning any database compromise would be catastrophic.

### 3. Data Retention Void
We saw data being collected for an emergency, but with no "self-destruct" date. Without a clear retention policy, temporary emergency data risks becoming a permanent liability.

## The Fallout
While we didn't see a "Marketplace Dump" on the dark web, the exposure risk was massive. Millions of citizens entrust the government with their health data, and this breach highlighted a glaring gap: Nepal effectively has no **Data Protection Act** to hold anyone accountable.

## The Lessons
1.  **Privacy by Design**: You can't bolt on security after you build the app—especially for health data.
2.  **Compliance Standards**: We need HIPAA-level standards in Nepal. "It works" is not a valid security certification.
3.  **Ephemeral Data**: Emergency systems should auto-delete data after 30 days. Why keep it forever?
