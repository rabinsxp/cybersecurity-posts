## The Incident
Tihar 2017. While Kathmandu was lit up for the festival of lights, hackers were busy lighting up the SWIFT network at NIC Asia Bank. In a brazen heist, they attempted to transfer **NPR 460 Million (USD 4.4 Million)** to accounts across six countries, including the US, UK, and Japan.

This was big. It wasn't an ATM hack; it was an attack on **SWIFT**—the global backbone of inter-bank money movement.

## Understanding the Attack
This wasn't a "smash and grab." It was a patient, calculated insider threat scenario (whether malicious insider or compromised credentials).

### The Methodology
The attack bore a striking resemblance to the infamous **2016 Bangladesh Bank Heist**.
1.  **The Entry**: Attackers compromised a PC on the SWIFT network. Rumors suggest it might have been a machine used for general email/browsing—a massive security sin.
2.  **The Credentials**: They didn't break the encryption; they stole the keys. They obtained valid operator credentials to initiate transfers.
3.  **Timing**: They waited for Tihar. Public holidays are the "Golden Hour" for attackers because human monitoring is at its lowest.
4.  **Cover Up**: Malware was used to wipe logs and potentially alter printed transaction confirmations, buying them time before the reconciliation team noticed the gap.

## The Response
Fortunately, the recovery was swift (pun intended). NIC Asia, coordinating with Nepal Rastra Bank and international partners, managed to freeze and claw back most of the funds before they could be cashed out. But the near-miss left the banking sector shaken.

## The Lessons
1.  **The Four-Eyes Principle**: No single human should be able to move millions. A transaction initiated by User A must always be physically approved by User B on a separate device.
2.  **Air Gapping**: A SWIFT terminal is for SWIFT. Period. It should not have access to Google, Facebook, or Corporate Email. If it can browse the web, it can be phished.
3.  **Automated Watchdogs**: Holidays are for humans, not security centers. Anomaly detection systems don't take Tihar off. They should have flagged a massive cross-border surge instantly.
