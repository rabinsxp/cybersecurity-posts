## The Heist
It plays out like a movie script. Over a single weekend in September 2019, Chinese threat actors coordinated a massive cash-out. They didn't blow up ATMs; they quietly withdrew over **NPR 1.89 Crore** from machines across Kathmandu.

The victim wasn't a single bank—it was the **Nepal Electronic Payment Systems (NEPS)**, the centralized switch that connects 17 commercial banks.

## Understanding the Attack
This wasn't simple card skimming. This was a sophisticated "Logical Attack."

### The Kill Chain
1.  **The Entry**: Weeks before the heist, attackers likely phished an employee or found an exposed server to plant a foothold in the corporate network.
2.  **The Pivot**: They moved laterally until they found the prize: the Payment Switch Application servers.
3.  **The Malware**: They installed a custom ISO8583 message interceptor.
4.  **The "Man-in-the-Middle"**:
    *   Mules at the ATM inserts a card.
    *   ATM asks the Switch: "Can I dispense 20k?"
    *   The Malware intercepts the request *before* it gets to the Core Banking System (CBS).
    *   Malware replies: "Yes, Dispense."
    *   The bank's legitimate ledger never even sees the transaction. The money vanishes, but the account balance stays the same.

## The Discovery
The only reason they got caught? A bank employee noticed their "Cash in Transit" vault accounts were emptying out way too fast for a Saturday. If they had gone slower, they might have cleaned out the entire vault.

## What Needs to Change
1.  **Air Gaps**: Your Payment Switch should not be reachable from the same network where HR checks their email. Segmentation is non-negotiable.
2.  **FIM Alerts**: Someone installed malware on the most critical server in the bank. Using File Integrity Monitoring would have alerted the SOC the second a new binary appeared in the folder.
3.  **Behavioral Friction**: Why is someone withdrawing the exact max limit from 60 different ATMs at 3 AM on a Saturday? Automated fraud detection rules need to block this, human or not.
