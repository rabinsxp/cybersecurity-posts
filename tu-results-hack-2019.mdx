## The Incident
In 2019, **Tribhuvan University (TU)**—Nepal's academic pride—faced a shameful scandal. The TU Service Commission website was compromised, but not to steal data. It was to *change* it.

Scores for competitive administrative exams were manipulated. Failing candidates suddenly passed; toppers were pushed down. This wasn't just a hack; it was corruption digitized.

## How They Did It
This centers on the **Integrity** pillar of security.

### The Attack Surface
1.  **The Insider Threat**: This likely wasn't an external teenager in a hoodie. The precision of the score changes suggests someone with knowledge of the database schema—either an insider with admin rights or an attacker who bought credentials from one.
2.  **Weak Access Controls**: The backend system likely allowed anyone with "Admin" status to edit a score field without triggering a permanent, unalterable log entry.
3.  **SQL Injection**: There's also a high probability of SQLi vulnerabilities in the legacy results portal, allowing direct database manipulation.

## The Fallout
*   **Credibility Crisis**: Thousands of students realized the "meritocracy" might be rigged.
*   **Legal Action**: The CIAA stepped in, filing corruption cases against top officials.
*   **Stalled Hiring**: The entire recruitment process froze for months, paralyzing university operations.

## The Fixes
1.  **Immutable Audit Logs**: You can't just let an admin change a score from "40" to "90" without a trace. Use a "Write Once, Read Many" (WORM) log or a blockchain ledger for high-stakes results.
2.  **The "Four Eyes" Rule**: Critical changes (like altering a final score) should require digital signatures from two independent officials.
3.  **Least Privilege**: Why does the web application need "WRITE" access to the historical results table? It should be Read-Only.
